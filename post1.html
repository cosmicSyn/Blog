Eric Fromm on Love

Dec 6th 2022 8 Min read

This post is a combination of my experience of the podcast and synthesis of the podcast content. Full credit to the host, Stephen West and the philosophers covered for the ideas outlined below. Content is organized into my themes and not necessarily the order/ chapters followed by the host.

Impressions

I initially thought that this would be a somewhat lighter listen. But then this had my desk packed with so many words. Can’t blame myself, there was so much to unpack. I loved this episode, cause it was packed with some amazing quotes, and makes you wonder on essential everyday choices of love that often go intuitively wrong.


How I Discovered It

Just scrolling through the episode list of ‘Philosophize This!’ on Spotify. Anything on love is instantly clickable. This is Episode 150 on Eric Fromm- Love.


Quick note on Eric Fromm

Eric Fromm is a 20th century German philosopher best known for his popular book “The Art of Loving”, his contributions to the Frankfurt School of thought and criticising the works of Freud. A man’s works give you so much more than what his biography (/quick note) would; so instead of the Wikipedia page on this guy, try reading his early works, maybe starting with the one I recommended- “Escape from freedom”.



Love is a answer. But ever asked why?


| Love is the only sane and satisfactory answer to the fundamental problem of human existence.


What he remarks from the starting quote as the fundamental problem; is separateness. The existential loneliness (linked to lack of meaning) and the feeling of alienation from everything and everyone that we walk with everyday; and the worst part is being conscious of it. This separateness is what causes us to search for endeavours that can make us something beyond our limited biological existence, something which we can leave behind after our death, hoping it to be part of a bigger picture. Thus, we arrive at religion, philosophy, creative work or perhaps love.



What form of love do you practice?

It is common to see that people have been starved of love. You see it themed in movies, sitcoms, music, books and what not (In fact people plan their entire life trajectory around find love).

  • Transactional love/ Pseudo love
  • Fromm’s conception of love


Conclusion

So it might help to think of your choices and actions in light of the bucket they fall into. Not like this’ll be a black and white, one all be all answer, but you might wanna ask yourself whether you are entering/ engaging in transactional model of love? Whether you love the person or the idea of that person? If love means so much to you, why not think about it a bit. You don’t wanna be stuck in an enlarged egotism without even knowing it.


So here’s a quick mental check I’ll leave you with- Are you going for monopoly or are you going for art?



My top quotes


What does one person give to another? He gives of himself, of the most precious he has, he gives of his life. This does not necessarily mean that he sacrifices his life for the other—but that he gives him of that which is alive in him; he gives him of his joy, of his interest, of his understanding, of his knowledge, of his humor, of his sadness—of all expressions and manifestations of that which is alive in him. In this giving of his life, he enriches the other person, he enhances the other's sense of aliveness by enhancing his own sense of aliveness. He does not give in order to receive; giving is in itself exquisite joy. But in giving he cannot help bringing something to life in the other person, and this which is brought to life reflects back to him.

Love is an activity, not a passive affect; it is a “standing in,” not a “falling for.” In the most general way, the active character of love can be described by stating that love is primarily giving, not receiving.
